Molecular Biology of SARS-CoV-2


Mechanism of Entry


SARS-CoV-2 initiates its contact with human cells through the use of spike (S) glycoproteins (Fig. 1) scattered across the outer-surface of the virion. There are two main pathways that SARS-CoV-2 may take to access the host cell:

  1. hACE-2 Receptor-mediated Endocytosis
  2. TMPRSS-2 Facilitated Membrane Fusion

The S glycoprotein will exhibit affinity with the human angeotensin-converting enzyme 2 (hACE-2), binding the receptor as the host cell initiates endocytosis - the uptake of particles into a cell. Once the virion enters the host cell, it is enclosed within a compartment of the host cell membrane referred to as the endosome. Within the acidic environment of the endosome, specific proteins known as Cathepsins (Fig. 3) are activated to cleave the S2' site (Fig. 2) of the S protein. Once the S2' site is cleaved, the fusion peptide (FP, Fig. 2) is exposed which readily changes the conformation of the S protein enabling membrane fusion with the endosome. This process results in releasing the SARS-CoV-2 genome into the host cell. 1.


Alternatively, transmembrane proteins commonly produced in cells of the human respiratory tract2 known as Transmembrane serine protease 2 (TMPRSS2, Fig. 4) readily cleave the S2' site of the spike protein at the surface of the cell. The fusion peptide is subsequently exposed, resulting in membrane fusion and release of the SARS-CoV-2 genome into the cell. 1.
